About This Project

One walk in the woods.
A lot of reading.
A neighborhood that decided to act.

How It Started

The forest was changing. Faster than it should.

One fall, walking through the property in the Pocono Mountains, I noticed more light than there used to be. More standing dead wood. The understory opening up in a way that felt less like a gap and more like a collapse. I knew beech bark disease had been around for decades, but this was different — faster, more complete.

Later I'd learn why. A second pathogen had arrived: Beech Leaf Disease, a nematode that kills infected trees in two to five years with no available forest-scale treatment. No resistant variety exists commercially. The beech component of these forests is, for practical purposes, done.

The question that kept coming back was simple: what fills those gaps? Left alone, the answer is red maple, hay-scented fern, and invasive shrubs. The diverse, multi-layered northern hardwood forest that grew here for centuries doesn't come back on its own — not with the deer pressure we have, not with invasives established in every opening. If nothing is planted intentionally, the forest that replaces it is a diminished one.

The Research

The answers are in the literature. Most people never find them.

So I started reading. Penn State Extension. The USFS Northern Research Station. Peer-reviewed literature on forest restoration in the Central Appalachians. What I found surprised me: the research on this exact forest type — northern hardwood at 1,800–2,200 feet in northeastern Pennsylvania — is specific, current, and actionable. Most of what it recommends doesn't require expensive equipment or professional contractors.

The science also changed what I thought I knew going in.

🌡️

Tubes aren't one-size-fits-all

A 2024 Penn State study found the interior of a plastic tree tube reaches 30°F above ambient on sunny days. That heat measurably harms sugar maple, eastern hemlock, and yellow birch — exactly the cool-adapted species most important at this elevation. PSU Extension 2024 →

🌰

Hickories are missing from most plans

Pre-settlement forests here were oak-hickory co-dominant. Modern restoration plantings have largely dropped the hickory half. Shagbark and pignut take 50–70 years to reach bat-roosting size — they need to be in the ground now, not later.

🗺️

Where you source matters as much as what you plant

USFS-backed research now recommends sourcing seedlings from 100–200 miles south at lower elevation — populations naturally adapted to the warmer conditions this site will experience in 2060–2080. You're planting for the next century, not today. Palik et al. 2022 →

These aren't intuitive conclusions. They're buried in research papers and extension bulletins. And if you don't find them, you spend real money on trees that underperform or fail for reasons that were entirely preventable.

There are also free programs most private landowners don't know exist: a DCNR service forester who will walk your property and review your plan at no charge, and federal cost-share through NRCS that covers 50–75% of qualifying restoration work. The barrier to doing this right is lower than it looks.

How It Grew

A personal project became a neighborhood one.

I shared what I'd found with some neighbors. We compared notes on what we were each seeing on our own properties — the same dying beech, the same deer pressure, the same gaps filling with the wrong species. It quickly became clear that it made more sense to do this together than separately.

Bulk bare-root seedling pricing drops dramatically at 100–300 trees: the difference between paying retail and paying a fraction of it. And a coordinated species mix across several adjacent properties is more ecologically coherent than any single property could achieve alone — more canopy diversity, more staggered food resources, more connected habitat for the wildlife that moves across all of it.

The research, the species list, the plans, the planner — all of it grew out of organizing that group effort and wanting to make sure we were doing it right.

This Site

Built to share the research, not gate it.

Every recommendation on this site is grounded in the same peer-reviewed literature and Penn State Extension research I found — with citations intact. Nothing is invented. Everything can be verified. The species guide, the planting plans, and the interactive planner are here to make it straightforward to go from "I see what's happening to my forest" to "I know what to plant and where."

If you're seeing what we're seeing — the dead beech, the opening canopy, the sense that the forest is changing faster than it should — this site is for you. The gaps are real. So is the opportunity they represent.
