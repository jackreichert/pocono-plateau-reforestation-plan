A forest is layers, not just trees

A healthy northern hardwood forest at this elevation has five distinct layers, each with its own ecological role. When you restore all of them, you rebuild the complete system — not just the part you can see from the road. Filter by layer below to learn what each one does and why it matters.

🌳 The Canopy Layer · 40–100ft

The roof of the forest — and its engine

Canopy trees are the structural backbone of the forest. They close the roof, regulate the microclimate below, and produce the mast — acorns, hickory nuts, berries — that feeds the entire food web from bears and turkeys to migratory songbirds and bats.

The dominant species here should be oaks and hickories. Pre-settlement forests on this plateau were oak-hickory co-dominant — not maple-dominated (Nowacki & Abrams 2008 →). Oaks support more wildlife species (500+) than any other eastern tree genus (Tallamy & Shropshire 2009 →). Hickories provide irreplaceable bat roosting habitat and hard mast that no other species can replace.

Why so many oak species?
Red oak for gaps (fast), white oak for longevity (centuries), chestnut oak for dry ridges, black oak as companion. Different niches, all native here.
Why hickories specifically?
Shagbark hickory's peeling bark is the primary roosting habitat for Indiana bats. A shagbark takes 50–70 years to reach bat-roosting size — plant it now for the next generation.
Use tubes for oaks, wire cages for maples
PSU 2024: tube interiors reach 30°F above ambient. Oaks tolerate it. Sugar maple, yellow birch, and black birch don't — use open wire cages for those.

🌿 The Understory Layer · 15–40ft

Where most of the daily wildlife action happens

The understory is the mid-story that grows beneath the canopy. These trees tolerate shade, provide the first spring bloom, summer berries for birds, and year-round nesting cover. For many forest birds, this is the layer they actually live in — not the tall canopy above.

Good news: some understory species spread naturally through bird dispersal and stump-sprouting. Serviceberry and hop-hornbeam often colonize within a few years — survey for them during your spring walk and protect what you find with wire cages. Focus your purchase budget on the ones that won’t arrive on their own.

First to bloom
Serviceberry blooms in late March — before anything else. Those early flowers are critical nectar for bees and early-emerging butterflies waking up from winter.
Last to bloom
Witch hazel flowers in October and November — after all the leaves fall. The forest equivalent of a surprise. Seeds explode audibly up to 30 feet.
Why pagoda dogwood instead of flowering dogwood?
Flowering dogwood suffers severe anthracnose disease in shaded, humid forest interiors at this elevation. Pagoda dogwood is disease-free and thrives in exactly these conditions.

🌲 The Conifer Layer · 40–80ft

Year-round structure — and you can't get it any other way

Conifers are the only layer that looks the same in February as in July. That year-round structure — dense branches even in winter — is critical thermal cover for deer, turkey, and songbirds when temperatures drop and food is scarce.

These must be planted. Red spruce and balsam fir have no natural seed source at 2,000ft on this plateau. Without active planting, this layer simply disappears from the forest. Once planted, they live for centuries and create a completely different microclimate in their shade.

Where to plant them
North-facing slopes and cool ravines for red spruce and balsam fir. Stream edges and shaded hollows for hemlock. Exposed dry ridges for pitch pine.
The hemlock situation
Hemlock Woolly Adelgid is confirmed in all PA counties. Hemlock must be treated every 4–6 years with a soil drench. It's worth it — nothing else creates the cool, dark stream-edge microhabitat hemlock provides.
Always wire cages for conifers
Conifers are cool-adapted and harmed by tube heat. Use open wire cages for every conifer you plant.

🌱 The Shrub Layer · 5–20ft

The layer that multiplies everything above it

A 2025 study in National Science Review found that increasing shrub diversity from 2 to 8 species boosted total tree biomass by 53.8% — nearly the same effect as adding more canopy tree species. Shrubs don't just fill space. They actively accelerate the growth of everything above them.

Shrubs also provide the densest nesting cover, the most varied food resources (early spring nectar, summer berries, winter fruit), and the movement corridors that connect habitat patches across the landscape.

Target 6–8 shrub species
The research finding is specifically about diversity, not just quantity. Six to eight different shrub species triggers the full biomass boost. One or two shrub species don't get you there.
Plant them loosely, not as a hedge
Scatter individual shrubs 6–8ft apart around the outer edges of your tree clusters. They're not a border — they're part of the understory fabric.

🍎 Fruiting & Flowering Trees · 15–80ft

The bonus layer — food and flowers

These trees were selected primarily for wildlife food value, seasonal flowers, or historical significance. They enrich the restoration without dominating it. The goal is to keep this layer at roughly 15% of your total — enough to add value, not enough to shift the ecological focus away from the oak-hickory backbone.

The native sweet crabapple and American chestnut deserve special mention: the crabapple is one of the most fragrant and wildlife-productive spring trees available, and the chestnut represents one of the most important ecological restoration stories in North American conservation history.

The American Chestnut story
A blight fungus arrived from Asia in 1904 and killed an estimated 4 billion trees within 50 years (Paillet 2002 →). TACF backcross hybrids have intermediate resistance. Planting one is an act of ecological restoration and historical memory.
Not Sargent crabapple
Malus coronaria is the native sweet crabapple — rose-scented, hosts 287 Lepidoptera species (Tallamy & Shropshire 2009 →), feeds birds through January. Sargent crabapple is a Japanese ornamental. They are not the same tree.

Lepidoptera species counts (moths & butterflies supported by each host plant) are from Tallamy, D.W. & Shropshire, K.J. (2009). "Ranking Lepidopteran Use of Native Versus Introduced Plants." Conservation Biology 23(4). doi.org/10.1111/j.1523-1739.2009.01202.x →
